Equity & Access

Technology should empower everyone, not just those with the resources or abilities that we often assume are standard. Throughout my time at UW, I've worked to break down barriers and create opportunities for underrepresented and underserved communities.

From teaching kids to code to building accessibility tools, these experiences have shaped my understanding of what it means to be an ethical and responsible technologist.

I've volunteered with several organizations that teach coding to K-12 students, particularly those from underrepresented backgrounds in tech. These programs introduce programming through creative projects like game design, robotics, and web development.

Impact: Worked with over 50 students across multiple workshops, many of whom had never written a line of code before. Seeing their excitement when they built their first game or website reminded me why representation in tech matters.

Reflection: Many of these students told me they didn't think "people like them" could be programmers. Breaking down those misconceptions and showing students they belong in tech has been one of the most meaningful parts of my college experience. Developed a browser extension that enhances web accessibility for users with visual impairments. Features include customizable text-to-speech, high-contrast modes, font size adjustment, and automatic image description generation using AI.

Impact: Used by over 200 people in beta testing, with feedback that it made previously inaccessible websites usable. One user told me the extension allowed them to independently browse news sites for the first time in years.

Reflection: This project taught me that accessibility is not an afterthought‚Äîit should be built into technology from the start. Working with users who rely on assistive technology opened my eyes to the countless barriers that exist in the digital world. Every website I build now, I think about accessibility first.

Technical Learning: Implemented ARIA labels, keyboard navigation, screen reader optimization, and semantic HTML. Learned about WCAG guidelines and the importance of testing with actual assistive technology users.

üéì Mentoring & Office Hours

Beyond formal TA responsibilities, I've made it a priority to create welcoming and inclusive spaces in my office hours and mentoring sessions. This includes being mindful of imposter syndrome, creating judgement-free environments, and actively encouraging students from underrepresented backgrounds.

Impact: Several students have told me that my office hours were the first time they felt comfortable asking "basic" questions without fear of judgment. Creating safe learning environments has helped students who might otherwise drop out of CS persist and succeed.

Reflection: Equity in education means recognizing that not all students start from the same place. Some students come to college with years of coding experience, while others are writing their first program. My job as a TA is to meet students where they are and help them grow, regardless of their starting point.

üîç Inclusive Design in Every Project

In all my Web Impact projects and personal work, I've made inclusive design a priority. This means considering diverse users from the start: people with disabilities, non-native English speakers, users with limited internet bandwidth, and those with varying levels of technical literacy.

Principles I Follow:

  • Design for screen readers and keyboard navigation from day one
  • Use clear, simple language and avoid jargon
  • Ensure color contrast meets WCAG standards
  • Optimize for slow connections and older devices
  • Provide multiple ways to accomplish tasks
  • Test with real users from diverse backgrounds

Reflection: Inclusive design makes products better for everyone, not just users with specific needs. Features built for accessibility‚Äîlike captions, keyboard shortcuts, and clear navigation‚Äîbenefit all users. This perspective has fundamentally changed how I approach software development.

Why Equity & Access Matter

Technology has incredible power to improve lives, but it can also reinforce existing inequalities if we're not intentional about who we're building for. I've learned that creating equitable technology requires:

  • Empathy: Understanding the diverse experiences and challenges users face
  • Representation: Ensuring diverse voices are part of the design process
  • Intentionality: Making accessibility and inclusion priorities, not afterthoughts
  • Education: Sharing knowledge and opportunities with underserved communities

These experiences embody the Husky 100 value of making a positive impact by removing barriers and creating opportunities for all people to thrive.
